FAQ

Does this indicator “predict” the future?

It does not forecast price directly. It measures how often certain slope sequences were followed by specific outcomes in the past and plots those probabilities for context.

What’s the difference between Raw Probability and Wilson ranking?

Raw ranking favors the highest observed win rate. Wilson lower‑bound ranking penalizes low sample sizes and favors patterns that are statistically more stable.

Why are some patterns marked “unstable”?

Unstable flags typically indicate too few occurrences or insufficient statistical confidence. Increase the minimum sample size or use Wilson ranking for more conservative results.

How many forward bars should I use?

Start small (ex: 1–5) to evaluate immediate follow‑through. Use larger values to study multi‑bar continuation, but expect more noise and fewer clean patterns.

Will it repaint?

Plotted probability blocks can update as new data arrives and as the pattern database grows. Once a bar is closed, its slope inputs are fixed; the statistical model can still evolve with more history.

Is it better on certain instruments or timeframes?

It works anywhere slopes are meaningful, but results are instrument/timeframe dependent. Use the CSV export to validate what patterns are robust for your specific market.

How do I avoid overfitting?

Use larger bucket sizes, require higher minimum sample sizes, and prefer Wilson ranking. Always validate on out‑of‑sample periods before relying on any pattern set.

What’s included in the CSV export?

Typically: the slope token sequence, occurrence count, forward probabilities (+1…+N), and any stability or confidence metric used for ranking.
